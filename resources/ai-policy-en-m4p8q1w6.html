1. Purpose and Scope

This document sets out the rules for using artificial intelligence (AI) tools within the organisation. The goal is not to block AI or create unnecessary friction. The goal is to ensure employees use AI in a way that complies with applicable law and does not expose the company or its clients to risk.

This policy is grounded in the following core regulatory frameworks:

  • EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) — applies from August 2026
  • GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) on personal data protection
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework 1.0 — voluntary, used as governance baseline
  • ISO 42001:2023 — AI management system standard
  • Local employment law and commercial law as applicable in each jurisdiction

Jurisdiction-specific frameworks (UK ICO, Korean AI Act) are covered in Appendix D.

This policy applies to all employees, contractors, and third parties with access to company systems or data who use AI tools in the course of their work.

2. Key Definitions

AI ProviderThe entity that develops or places an AI system on the market (e.g. Anthropic, Microsoft, OpenAI). Bears primary responsibility for the model's compliance with the EU AI Act.
AI DeployerThe company using an AI system in its own operations — your organisation. Responsible for how the tool is used, which tool is selected, and oversight of outputs.
AI UserAn individual (employee) interacting with an AI tool in day-to-day work.

Trade secretInformation with commercial value that is not publicly available and is actively protected by the company (pricing, margins, strategy, know-how).

High-risk AIAI system used in areas with significant impact on people — employment, education, credit, justice. Subject to stricter oversight requirements under EU AI Act.

AI agentAutonomous AI system capable of completing tasks and taking actions without direct human oversight at every step.
Human-in-the-loopA governance principle requiring a human to review and approve AI outputs before they are acted upon, particularly for binding or high-impact decisions.
Note on EU AI Act roles: Your organisation is the deployer. The provider is Anthropic, Microsoft, etc. As deployer, you are responsible for: selecting appropriate tools, ensuring human oversight, maintaining records in high-risk use cases, and not using AI in prohibited ways.

6. General Rules of Use

6.1 Always applicable

  • AI outputs are a starting point, not a final product. Any output going outside the company must be reviewed by a human.
  • AI must not be the sole basis for decisions with direct impact on the rights of others — human review is required.
  • If communicating with a customer or partner using AI-generated content, this must be transparent — either through labelling or prior approval.
  • Never input login credentials, passwords, API keys or tokens into any AI tool.
  • Binding documents (orders, contracts, offers) must not be sent without human approval, even if AI assembled them.

6.2 Copyright and ownership of AI outputs

  • AI-generated outputs are not automatically protected by copyright — they lack the human creative element required for protection under most jurisdictions.
  • AI-generated content intended for publication, commercial use, or incorporation into contracts must undergo human editing and review.
  • Prohibited: Inputting third-party texts, images or code into AI without appropriate licence.
  • Outputs produced by employees using AI in the course of their work belong to the employer — consistent with standard work-for-hire provisions.

6.3 Microsoft Copilot

  • Copilot operates within the M365 tenant. Data stays in the tenant; covered by DPA with Microsoft.
  • Copilot can access everything you have access to. Be conscious of what you include in a prompt.
  • Exception: health data and other special categories (GDPR Art. 9) — do not belong in Copilot even within the tenant.

6.4 AI agents and automation

  • Every deployed AI agent must be documented (see Appendix A).
  • Agents must not autonomously send communications to customers or third parties without human approval.
  • Agents processing personal data must have a documented legal basis for processing.

7. Regulatory Framework — Practical Summary

7.1 EU AI Act — Deployer obligations

As deployer, the organisation must:

  • Use AI systems in accordance with the provider's instructions and for the intended purpose.
  • Implement human oversight of AI outputs, especially in regulated domains.
  • Maintain records of AI use in high-risk contexts.
  • Not deploy AI for prohibited uses (Art. 5): social scoring, subliminal manipulation, real-time biometric identification in public spaces, AI decisions based on race, religion, political opinion or sexual orientation.
High-risk area: Use of AI to assist in supplier selection, employee performance assessment or recruitment falls within the regulated zone. Requirements: human oversight, transparency, documentation.

7.2 GDPR

  • DPA with AI provider is a prerequisite for transferring personal data. Verify that signed agreements are in place.
  • Automated decision-making (Art. 22): Individuals have the right not to be subject to decisions based solely on automated processing with legal or significant effects.
  • Right to explanation: Where AI assists in a decision about a person, that person has the right to understand how the decision was reached.
  • Data minimisation: Only input data that is strictly necessary for the purpose at hand.

7.3 NIST AI RMF 1.0 (governance baseline)

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework provides a voluntary structure for managing AI risks across four functions:

GOVERN

This policy. Roles, responsibilities, risk appetite, decision-making authority for AI deployment.

MAP

Identify which AI tools are used, what risks they carry, and how they affect people and processes.

MEASURE

Assess AI outputs regularly for accuracy, bias, reliability. Document findings.

MANAGE

Act on identified risks — update, restrict, or retire tools. Annual review minimum.

7.4 ISO 42001:2023

ISO 42001 is the international standard for AI Management Systems. Key principles applied in this policy:

  • Risk-based approach: AI tools are evaluated before deployment based on risk to individuals and the organisation.
  • Accountability: Clear ownership for each AI tool and AI-assisted process.
  • Continual improvement: Annual policy review and incident-driven updates.
  • Transparency: Employees and affected parties are informed about AI use.
✏ CUSTOMISE — If pursuing ISO 42001 certification, additional documentation requirements apply. Consult your certification body.

7.5 Trade secrets and confidential information

Trade secrets include: pricing and margin data, spend analytics, sourcing strategies, supplier and contract terms, unpublished financial results.

  • This data must not be entered into any external AI tool — even in anonymised form if the combination could enable identification.
  • Breach of trade secret protection may give rise to civil and, in some jurisdictions, criminal liability.

7.6 Liability for AI-assisted decisions

  • The organisation, as deployer, is liable for harm caused by AI outputs that were used without adequate human review.
  • The EU AI Liability Directive (in adoption process) will introduce facilitated burden of proof for AI-related harm claims.
  • Practical implication: Every decision with external impact must be human-verified and documented.

Appendix D

Jurisdiction-Specific Addenda

Remove the sections below if your organisation does not operate in these jurisdictions and does not process data of residents in these countries.

Part 1: United Kingdom Applicable where the company operates in the UK or processes data of UK residents

Regulatory basis: UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018, enforced by the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO). UK ICO has published specific AI guidance that supplements the core data protection obligations.

  • UK GDPR applies to UK data subjects and UK operations post-Brexit. Largely mirrors EU GDPR but is independently enforced by the ICO.
  • ICO's AI guidance emphasises: fairness, transparency, and accountability in automated processing.
  • AI systems used in employment or customer-facing decisions must be explainable to affected individuals.
  • High-risk AI processing may require a Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA).
  • If the organisation transfers personal data from the UK to EEA or other countries, appropriate transfer mechanisms must be in place (UK adequacy decisions or UK International Data Transfer Agreements).
Part 2: South Korea Applicable where the company operates in South Korea or processes data of Korean residents

Regulatory basis: Act on Development of AI and Establishment of Trust (Korean AI Act, enacted 2024) and Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA).

  • High-impact AI (similar to EU AI Act high-risk category) must implement safeguards and human oversight.
  • Transparency obligations: Users must be informed when interacting with AI — disclosure is mandatory for customer-facing AI applications.
  • Data governance: Personal data used for AI training or processing must comply with PIPA. Separate consent obligations apply.
  • Providers of AI services in Korea must register with the Korea Communications Commission if meeting volume thresholds.
  • Cross-border data transfers require additional safeguards under PIPA — ensure data processing agreements explicitly cover cross-border flows.
